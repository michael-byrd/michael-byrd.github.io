In this post, we explore the fundamentals of group theory.

What is a group anyway?

A group is a set \(G\) equipped with an operation \( \star \) that satisifies four axioms.

  • A group is closed under the operation \(\star\). That is, for all \(a, b \in G\), we have \(a \star b \in G \).
  • A group is associative. That is, for all \(a,b,c \in G\), we have \(a \star (b \star c) = (a \star b) \star c\).
  • A group has an identity, \( e \in G \) such that \( e \star a = a \star e = a, \) for all \(a \in G.\)
  • Every element in the group has an inverse. That is, for all \(a \in G\), there exists an element, say \(b\) such that \( a \star b = b \star a = e \).

Some examples of groups include: the set of integers, symmetries of a regular polygon, and the numbers on a clock.

Integers

The set of integers, \(\mathbb{Z}\), form a group under the operation addition.

  • Given two integers, \(x\) and \(y\), we have that \(x+y\) is also an integer.
  • The integers are associative under addition.
  • The element \(0\) satisifies the identity property, that is, \(x + 0 = 0 + x = x,\) for all \(x \in \mathbb{Z}\).
  • Every integer has an additive inverse. Given an element \( x \in \mathbb{Z}, \) the element \(-x \in \mathbb{Z},\) satisifes \( x + (-x) = (-x) + x = 0.\)

Symmetries of a triangle.

The set of symmetries of a triangle (or any regular polygon) form a group under the operation composition. We will consider these symmetries in two categories, rotations and flips.


Since we are working with a triangle, we have that counter-clockwise rotations by multiples of \(120\) degrees are symmetric operations. If we rotate a triangle by 0 degrees, we essentialy leave the triangle unchanged, which can be thought of as the identity operation, we will denote this \(e\).
Let the element \(r\) represent the counter-clockwise rotation of \(120\) degrees. Note that operation \( r \circ r\) is a rotation by \(240\) degrees, we will denote this \(r^2.\) Rotating a third time will return the triangle to its original state, therefore \(r^3 = r^2 \circ r = e\). Using this identity, we have that the only distinct rotations are \(e, r, \text{and} r^2\).
